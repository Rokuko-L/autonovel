LOG ENTRY: DAY 7

Every morning at 7:42 AM, the clock in the east hallway chimes thirteen times. No one talks about it. The teachers pretend not to hear. If you're caught looking at the clock when it chimes, you vanish by third period.

I've been counting. The first student disappeared on Day 1 — Marcus Chen, sat two rows ahead of me in homeroom. The bell rang, he looked up at the clock, and by lunch he was gone. The office said he "transferred." His desk was cleared by 1 PM. New kid sat there by 2.

No one remembered him except me.

I started keeping this log because I think the memory loss spreads. Sarah knew Marcus — we all did. But by Day 3 when I mentioned his name, she blinked and said "Who?" Not lying. Genuinely blank. Like he never existed.

The clock doesn't just take you. It erases you retroactively. Your seat refills. Your grades get reassigned. Your parents forget they had a child.

I think the clock is full. I think it's been full for a while and that's why the new disappearances are happening faster. Last week: one student every three days. This week: two.

Tomorrow is Day 8. The clock is in room 217, behind the locked door marked "AV CLOSET — STAFF ONLY." I know because I picked the lock during Dr. Hendricks's fifth period lecture on quadratic equations (she didn't notice — she never does anymore; her lessons trail off into silence and she stares at the wall).

I'm going in tomorrow. I'm bringing this log, a crowbar, and my phone (recording, voice memo, 1:37 AM timestamp — proving I existed if I don't come back).

If you're reading this, I didn't make it. Don't look at the clock. Don't say my name. You'll forget me anyway.

But maybe — maybe — read this out loud. Say my name once. If someone remembers, the clock doesn't win.

My name is Jamie Park. I was in Mr. Okonkwo's homeroom. I sat by the window.

I liked the light there.
