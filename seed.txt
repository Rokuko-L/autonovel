The clock in Homeroom 3B is the only one that works.

Not the wall-mounted digital, which flickers between frozen times like a seizure. Not the whiteboard timer, which melted into a puddle of black plastic last September. But the antique Regulator on the back wall, its brass pendulum swinging with the slow, ponderous weight of a heart full of mud. It has a hairline crack across its glass face, and the hands are shaped like barbed fishhooks. It reads 7:42 AM. It always reads 7:42 AM.

The first “erasure” was the janitor, Mr. Bellamy. He was fixing a fluorescent light in the gym’s crawlspace during the 7:42 AM bell. When the crew went to find him, there was no body. The crawlspace was empty. The only trace of him was a single, still-smoking work boot resting neatly on a folded towel, as if he had simply unzipped himself from existence. The school called it a “translocation anomaly.” The students call it “going fish-hook.”

The next was a sophomore named Leo Castell. He was texting in his seat. At 7:42:10, his phone clattered to the floor. Leo was gone. His chair was still warm. The school’s solution was to weld the doors of Homeroom 3B shut and re-route the morning bell. But the erasures didn’t stop. They just happened in the hallways, in the library, in the gym during woodwind practice. The clock is the heart, and the heart still beats. 7:42 AM. Always.

The protagonist is Amina Osei, a senior with a 4.0 and a diagnosed “hyper-associative memory.” Her flaw isn’t a lack of intelligence, but a flaw of *application*. She sees patterns in everything—the turn of a leaf, the frequency of a cough, the sequence of felled student IDs on the hallway floor—but she has never learned to *select*. She hoards data, drowning in connections, paralyzed by the choice of which thread to pull. The world doesn’t need a librarian, her mother always says. It needs a firefighter.

Her goal is to find her little brother, Theo. He was the last one taken, three days ago, during a fire drill. He vanished from the middle of a laughing group of friends. No smoke, no scream. Just a faint scent of ozone and a silence that felt sharp enough to draw blood. The others have forgotten him. The attendance roster is clean, his desk reassigned. Only Amina remembers, because Amina remembers *everything*.

She has spent those three nights staring at the clock, watching the fishhook hands of 7:42, her brain a pinball machine of data. The pattern she has finally isolated is this: the clock doesn’t erase people. It *selects* them. The time on the clock face is not a moment of day, but a fixed point in a cycle. She noticed the blue flower pollen on the pedestal. The only plant that blooms that particular blue in this hemisphere is a rare orchid that grows two hours south of here. The clock is a lock. The time is a key.

She has the data. Now she needs the nerve. This morning, at 7:42 AM, she plans to stand directly in front of the clock. She will not try to break its glass. She will not run. She will let the fishhook hand brush her shoulder. Because she has realized the only way to find Theo is to go exactly where he went. She just has to choose the right thread.
