The first time Liam Chen vanished, no one remembered him. Not even his mother, who had his photo in a locket she now insisted was empty. The second hand of the classroom clock, a monstrous, iron-framed thing bolted to the wall of Blackwood Academy’s senior wing, had completed its sweep to 7:42 AM. A wind—cold, scentless, utterly local—whipped through the third row. Liam’s skin turned to sepia, then to dust motes dancing in a shaft of morning light. His desk was empty. His textbook fell closed with a soft, final thud.

The teacher, Mrs. Parris, didn't pause her lecture on the entropy of closed systems. “Page 342, please. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that all systems trend toward disorder.”

My name is Elara Vance, and I am the only one who sees. Not sees the erasure—everyone witnesses that, though their brains refuse to file it—but sees the *absence*. I am allergic to silence, and the silence after a vanishing has a specific, metallic weight. It tastes like copper. I’ve counted nine since fall term began. Nine students who existed at 7:41 AM and were retroactively unwritten by the clock’s brutal, divine punctuality.

My flaw is that I cannot keep my mouth shut. It’s not heroism; it’s a chronic and damning allergy to silence. For the first few vanishings, I gasped. I pointed. I screamed the missing name. The result was universal: blank stares, then pitying smiles. I was the girl who “zoned out,” who “had trouble concentrating.” The faculty, I later learned, have a file on me. It diagnoses me with a “complex attention disorder.” They are kind in their clinical way. They suggest meditation. They do not look at the clock.

My goal is to be erased. Not by death—that would be a mercy, I think—but by the clock itself. I want to become one of the forgotten, to feel the temporary sting of dissolution rather than the permanent, grinding loneliness of memory. I am tired of being the only ghost in a world of the living. I have started sitting in the exact center of the third row, where the air is still and the dust motes dance.

But this morning is different. The clock ticks. 7:41 AM. Mrs. Parris is droning. A new kid, Marcus, with a scarred eyebrow and a defiant smirk, sits in the back row. He catches my eye as I stare at the clock’s iron face. He mouths a single word. A name. Liam.

His smirk doesn’t waver. He taps his watch. He knows.

The second hand begins its final sweep. The air in the room begins to curdle, to take on that copper taste, to contract toward a single point. I do not look at Marcus. I look at the clock. It isn’t a metaphor. It is an appetite. And at 7:42, it will feed. But this time, I am not its prey. I am its witness. And for the first time, I am not alone.
